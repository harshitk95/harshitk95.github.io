Music & the flute

Playing the Blues on a Bansuri

Bending an Indian classical flute into a blues scale - the blue note, half-holing, and scale shifts.

Blog · music

The bansuri and the blues grew up worlds apart, but they share a secret: both live in the spaces between the notes. Where a piano can only step from one key to the next, a bamboo flute can slide, bend and cry - exactly the vocabulary the blues is built on. This is a short guide to coaxing a blues scale out of a bansuri, and then using an old Indian trick to play it in any key.

01 The blues scale

The minor blues scale is six notes. Relative to the tonic (call it "1"), the degrees are:

  • 1 - the root
  • ♭3 - the minor third
  • 4 - the perfect fourth
  • ♭5 - the blue note, the soul of the sound
  • 5 - the perfect fifth
  • ♭7 - the minor seventh

In C that spells C – E♭ – F – G♭ – G – B♭. The ♭5 is the note that doesn't quite belong - you don't land on it, you pass through it, or bend into and out of it.

02 Mapping it onto swaras

A bansuri is naturally a major scale instrument - with all six holes open-then-closed it plays Bilawal (the Western major scale). To get the blues we borrow the komal (flat) swaras and one tivra (sharp) swara. Here is the full map, taking the flute's fundamental as Sa:

Blues degreeSwaraHow you get it
1SaFundamental - all relevant holes closed
♭3komal GaHalf-hole (partly uncover)
4shuddha MaStandard fingering
♭5tivra MaThe blue note - half-hole / bend
5PaStandard fingering
♭7komal NiHalf-hole (partly uncover)

So the blues scale in sargam reads: Sa – komal Ga – Ma – tivra Ma – Pa – komal Ni – Sa.

03 Half-holing & meend

The komal and tivra swaras above aren't fingered like piano keys - you get them by half-holing: partially covering a finger-hole so the pitch drops (or rises) by a semitone. Roll the finger, don't lift it. This is also the doorway to the most blues-like gesture on the flute: the meend, a continuous glide from one note to the next. Instead of jumping from Pa down to komal Ga, gradually roll your fingers off the holes and let the pitch bend through everything in between.

Practically: land on Ma, then bend up towards tivra Ma (the blue note) and fall back - that little waver is the whole feeling of the blues in a single breath.

04 Scale shifts - playing in any key

A bansuri is a fixed-key instrument: a C flute wants to play in C. But Indian music has a beautiful escape hatch called murchana - the movable Sa. The idea: pick a different hole to treat as your new Sa (tonic), and the same finger-patterns now spell a scale in a new key.

For the blues this is gold. If your Sa is the flute's fundamental, you're playing blues in the flute's home key. Shift Sa up to what was Pa (the fifth), keep the same relative blues fingerings from there, and you're now playing the blues a fifth higher - on the exact same flute. Each hole you nominate as Sa gives you a new blues key, at the cost of a slightly different tessitura and a few more half-holes to manage.

  • Sa = fundamental → home-key blues, easiest fingering.
  • Sa = Ma → blues up a fourth.
  • Sa = Pa → blues up a fifth, bright and open.

The trade-off is real: the further your new Sa sits from the flute's natural tonic, the more notes need half-holing, so intonation gets harder. Start with a shift to Pa - it's the friendliest - and grow from there.

05 A lick to start with

Try this slow, breathy phrase - all in the home key, using meend between the marked notes:

  • komal Ni → Sa (glide up into the root)
  • komal Ga → Ma (bend up)
  • Ma → tivra Ma → Pa (through the blue note and resolve)
  • Pa → komal Ga → Sa (long fall home)

Play it like you're sighing, not spelling. The blues isn't in the notes - it's in the bends between them, which is exactly what a bansuri was born to do.
